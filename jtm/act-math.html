Atomic Constraint Theory -- the math, explained

ACT tracks a job as one pile of small things and a few kinds of links between them. This lays out the math plainly. The point: four of ACT's rules -- one parent per atom, no numeric lag, cleared not claimed, constraints are yes/no -- are not taste. They are the rules that make the counting add up.

The "clock" is just a number: a day count on a live job. Everything else is defined as it comes up.

1. Atoms

An atom is one tracked thing: a task, a hold point, a delivery, a permit, a pour. You build an atom by adding fields one at a time -- "stacking traits." No class inheritance; every atom carries its own fields.

2. The three kinds of links

  • Contains -- the breakdown tree. Every atom sits under one parent. Carries rollups (Section 5).
  • After -- which atom waits on which. No circular waits. Carries dates and critical path (Section 6).
  • Tags -- free labels. For slicing the job; carry no math.

Anything that belongs in two places becomes a tag, never a second parent. Section 5 is why.

3. When is something done

Five kinds: trivial (born done), leaf (someone flips it), gate (done when its after-list is done), rollup (done when all children done), verified (done when a prover is done). Constraints are yes/no.

Compute the done set by iterating: mark atoms whose rule is satisfied, repeat until nothing new marks. The settled set is the least fixed point.

Loops can't cheat. A ring of gates each waiting on the next can never start -- they all stay open.

4. Progress never goes backwards

Claim. Move the clock forward and the done set only grows.

Why. The clock only enters via leaf clearings. Pushing the clock later can flip open to cleared, never the reverse.

5. Rollups: quantity and percent complete

percent complete = earned / budget

Why one parent: no double-counting. Each atom under a parent sits under exactly one child, so piles are separate and cover everything. A second parent creates a diamond where atoms land in both piles -- percent sails past 100%.

Cleared, never claimed. Progress counts only with evidence -- a leaf cleared by a ticket, a rollup whose children are themselves cleared.

6. Dates: the critical path

finish(a) = duration(a) + max(finish of everything a waits on)

The longest chain is the critical path. This is exactly CPM.

No numeric lag. Replace "+3 days" on an arrow with a real 3-day atom (concrete cure). Every date comes out identical; the wait now has a name, an owner, and a clock.

7. Durations are ranges

Run the schedule many times, drawing durations from ranges. Publish the spread, not a single date. A single-date plan built on averages runs short because merge points leak time.

Shared bad luck. Draw shared factors (weather, labour shortage) once per run, not per atom -- keeps related tasks slipping together.

8. History is just the running

Every change is a message. The board at any moment is the replay up to that moment. To see last Tuesday, stop the replay at Tuesday.

9. What is workable right now

The workable atoms: not yet done, entire after-list done. Sort by owner for the 6am view: what is choking the next stretch, and who can clear it.

10. Risk sits to the side

A risk threatens rather than blocks. It feeds the date ranges -- with its chance, the work it threatens slips by its impact. Blocking is hard and binary; threatening is soft and likely.

11. The old tools are special cases

  • CPM -- the "after" walk. Critical path = zero-float chain.
  • WBS + EV -- breakdown tree + weighted rollup.
  • AWP / Last Planner -- gates + workable list.
  • Theory of Constraints -- binding constraint = last atom on critical path.
  • Event sourcing -- the replay.

13. Things pushing in from outside

Crowding, weather, clash, radiation are all one thing: a field the atom reads through its box, spending a budget that controls when it may work -- never whether it is done.

A field someone invents next year works the instant a box reads it. ACT does not fix the list.

The six that carry the weight

  • Done set settles; loops can't cheat (S3)
  • Progress never goes backwards (S4)
  • One parent = percent adds up (S5)
  • Banning numeric lag loses nothing (S6)
  • Single-date plan on averages runs short (S7)
  • History = replay (S8)

One thing -- the atom. Three links -- contains, after, tag. The math is what each link carries, and ACT's rules keep it straight.
